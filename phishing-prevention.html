Phishing Prevention

Identify and avoid phishing attacks before they trick you

Why Phishing Prevention Matters

Phishing is one of the most common and successful cyber attacks. Attackers impersonate legitimate organizations to deceive you into revealing passwords, financial information, or other sensitive data. According to security reports, phishing attacks succeed 3% of the time - but attackers send millions, so they still compromise hundreds of thousands of victims.

A single phishing click can lead to identity theft, financial loss, ransomware infection, or unauthorized account access. Learning to recognize phishing is critical.

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a social engineering technique where attackers impersonate trustworthy entities (banks, PayPal, Amazon, etc.) to trick you into:

  • Revealing passwords or usernames
  • Entering credit card or banking information
  • Downloading and executing malware
  • Clicking malicious links
  • Visiting fake websites that steal credentials
  • Revealing personal information for identity theft

Key Security Practices

1

Check Sender Email Address

Verify the full email address, not just the display name. Scammers often fake display names (e.g., showing "Amazon" but the actual email is "amazon@suspicious-domain.com"). Hover over the sender to see the real address.

✅ Legitimate: support@amazon.com
❌ Phishing: support@amazo-n.com or amazon@secure-verify.com
2

Hover Over Links First

Never click links in emails from unknown senders or unexpected sources. Hover over the link (without clicking) to see where it actually points. Phishing links often point to fake websites that look real.

✅ Do: Hover to preview URL before clicking
❌ Don't: Click any link without verification
3

Never Enter Passwords via Email

This is THE most important rule. Legitimate companies NEVER ask for passwords by email, phone, or chat. If someone asks for your password, it's 100% a scam. Real companies use secure login pages.

✅ Safe: Log in directly to the website
❌ Phishing: Any email asking for password
4

Report Suspicious Emails

Use your email provider's report/spam feature to flag phishing attempts. Gmail, Outlook, and Yahoo have built-in phishing reporting. This helps protect other users and improves spam filters.

✅ Do: Click "Report phishing" in Gmail
❌ Don't: Respond to or engage with phishing
5

Watch for Urgent Language

Phishing emails often use urgency and fear to bypass critical thinking ("Act now!", "Verify immediately!", "Account suspended!"). Legitimate companies give you reasonable time to respond.

❌ Red flags: "Click here immediately", "Verify now", "Urgent action required"
✅ Normal: "Please update your information at your convenience"
6

Check for Poor Grammar

Many phishing emails contain spelling, grammar, and formatting errors. Legitimate companies proofread their communications. Poor English or awkward phrasing is a red flag.

❌ Phishing: "Confrim your acccount immediately!"
✅ Legitimate: "Please confirm your account information"
7

Verify Requests Independently

If an email claims to be from your bank, don't click any links. Instead, call the phone number on your bank card or card statement directly. Verify that the bank actually sent the email.

✅ Do: Call bank directly using trusted number
❌ Don't: Use phone number from the email
8

Look for Generic Greetings

Phishing emails often use generic greetings like "Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name. Legitimate companies typically personalize emails with your real name.

❌ Phishing: "Dear Customer," "Hello User,"
✅ Legitimate: "Hello John," "Dear Sarah Smith,"

Common Phishing Targets

  • Banks and Financial Institutions - Fake login pages to steal banking credentials
  • Email Providers - Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo - "Verify your account"
  • Online Retailers - Amazon, eBay - "Update payment method"
  • Streaming Services - Netflix, Hulu - "Confirm your subscription"
  • Payment Systems - PayPal, Stripe - "Unusual activity detected"
  • Social Media - Facebook, Twitter - "Confirm identity"
  • Tax Services - IRS impersonation during tax season
  • IT Support - Pretending to be Microsoft or Apple support

Types of Phishing Attacks

Email Phishing

Most common type. Fake emails impersonating legitimate companies with links to fake login pages.

Spear Phishing

Targeted phishing using personal information (name, company) to appear more legitimate.

SMS Phishing (Smishing)

Phishing via text messages. Links to fake apps or websites that steal credentials.

Voice Phishing (Vishing)

Telephone calls impersonating banks or IT support requesting personal information.

Whaling

Targeted phishing of high-value targets (CEOs, executives) with sophisticated attacks.

Clone Phishing

Attacker clones a legitimate email and replaces links/attachments with malicious ones.

Red Flags - Warning Signs

  • URL is slightly different from legitimate site (amazon.cmo instead of amazon.com)
  • Email requests unusual information (passwords, social security numbers, credit cards)
  • Site design is poor quality with broken images or misaligned text
  • Grammar and spelling errors throughout the email or website
  • Sender email address doesn't match the company domain
  • Unexpected attachments or links in emails
  • Request to confirm information you already provided
  • Mismatched sender information (claims to be from bank but domain is different)
  • Links that point to different domains than displayed
  • Requests to bypass normal security procedures

If You Fall for a Phishing Attack

  • Change your password immediately on that account
  • Contact the company's official support line (not from the email)
  • Monitor your account for unauthorized activity
  • Check credit reports for identity theft signs
  • If financial information was compromised, contact your bank
  • Report the phishing email to the company and email provider
  • Run antivirus/malware scans if you downloaded anything
  • Consider enabling two-factor authentication on all accounts
  • Place fraud alerts with credit bureaus if identity theft occurred
